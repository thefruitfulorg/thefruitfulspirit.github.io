Founder Story

I spent decades building technology for some of the largest financial companies in the world. I was good at it. I built systems, solved complex problems, and climbed the ladder that the world told me was worth climbing. But something was missing.

I grew up attending Christian schools and going to church on Sundays — well, sometimes. I knew the stories, sang the songs, and checked the boxes. But somewhere between childhood and career, faith became something I carried in my back pocket rather than something that carried me. I lived exactly how Proverbs warns us not to: I leaned entirely on my own understanding. I trusted my intellect, my work ethic, and my ability to solve problems above all else. I never formally walked away from God — I just stopped walking toward Him, distracted by the noise of my own ambition.

Even then, God was trying to reach me. My wife would often speak about the Fruit of the Spirit, and we even had art hanging in our home listing them out. I walked past those words every single day—Love, Joy, Peace...—but I didn't truly see them. To me, they were just decoration, pretty calligraphy on a canvas. My eyes were open, but my heart wasn't yet ready to listen.

When I started feeling that familiar restlessness — the sense that there must be more to life than what I'd built — I did what most people do. I turned to the self-help aisle — reading books about being a better person, listening to hours of podcasts, applying principles and systems. Productivity frameworks. Habit trackers. Morning routines designed to "prime" the mind and body for productivity, focusing on intentional, consistent, and proactive habits. I was searching for a better version of myself using every tool the secular world had to offer.

And then one day, the question hit me with a clarity I wasn't expecting:

If I truly wanted to become a better person, why would I look anywhere other than Jesus Christ?

It was that simple — and that disruptive. Here I was reading books on becoming the best version of myself, when the greatest model for how to live had been right in front of me my entire life. Not a framework. Not a formula. A person. The person who made the lame walk, the blind see, and showed us what it looks like to love without limits.

Coming back to church changed everything. I walked into Rockharbor Church, and as I sat in the chairs, I knew God had placed Pastor Glenn Packiam in my life for this exact moment. I heard him talk about how each person has a mission — calling us to something greater than ourselves. I began reading his book, What's a Christian, Anyway?, and words I'd heard a hundred times before landed differently. This time, they landed in a heart that was finally ready to receive them.

"We love because He first loved us."

— 1 John 4:19 (NIV)

As I dove deeper into scripture — inspired by works like Cultivating the Fruit of the Spirit by Christopher J. H. Wright — I discovered the Fruits of the Spirit: love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control. Nine words that together paint the most beautiful portrait of what it means to live like Jesus. Not through striving or willpower, but through letting the Holy Spirit do what only He can do: transform us from the inside out.

And here's what surprised me most: the peace and joy I found in this journey surpassed anything my career had ever given me. Not the temporary happiness of a promotion or the thrill of a successful launch — but a deep, unshakeable sense that I was finally doing what I was made to do.

That's when I realized something else. Those decades in technology weren't wasted time. They were preparation. God had been equipping me all along — giving me the skills, the experience, and the understanding of how technology can reach people — so that when this moment came, I'd be ready to answer the call.

But hearing that call didn't mean I was immediately ready to walk it out. When the revelation hit me that God actually wanted me to build this, I froze. The doubts flooded in instantly. Who am I to do this? I don't have a theology degree. I've spent my life building financial systems, not ministries. I wrestled with that feeling of complete inadequacy, worried that I wasn't equipped, holy enough, or ready for what He was asking. It wasn't until I realized that God doesn't call the equipped—He equips the called—that I was able to surrender the outcome and simply take the next step. I didn't need to have all the answers; I just needed to say yes.

If I can just help one person experience the same joy and peace the Holy Spirit has given me, then I would have succeeded.

That's not a tagline. That's the honest truth of my heart. I don't need millions of downloads or viral moments. I need to know that what God put on my heart reached someone else's.

I believe this is the point in my life where God is calling me to use everything He's given me — the years in technology, the experience building things that scale, the understanding of how people engage with digital tools — to serve Him and to be a light to others.

But let me be clear: I do not have it all figured out. In this journey of cultivating the Fruit of the Spirit, I often feel like a baby taking his first steps—learning, stumbling, and starting fresh every morning. I know that Jesus was perfect, and in my own strength, I could never come close to that standard. My hope is simply to become a fraction of who He is—to reflect just a spark of His light. So I am not offering you a map from someone who has arrived; I am offering you a hand from someone who is learning to walk right beside you.

And so, I make this my prayer every morning, borrowing the words of John Stott:

"Heavenly Father, I pray that this day I may live in your presence and please you more and more. Lord Jesus, I pray that this day I may take up my cross and follow you. Holy Spirit, I pray that this day you will fill me with yourself and cause your fruit to ripen in my life: love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control."

Growing. One fruit at a time.
